My role

My job as an Experience Design Consultant intern was to work on the conceptualization of TLSbiometrics. I worked within team composed of a product manager, another UX designer and a handful of developers from my company and the client's company. There is also a UI designer that joined us at the end of the conceptualization phase.

The challenge

Process more visa applicants

Our ultimate goal for the project was to process more visa applicants (TLScontact's customers). Until then, TLScontact had not developed their own software to capture biometric data. With time, they noticed that the main productivity problem was the biometrics step, particularly because of the software, this is why they decided to create their own tailored product.

The research

Observing the visa agents work

Before jumping on a solution, we had to get accustomed to how the visa agents work. I went in a visa application center with two of my teammates to conduct ethnographic research, we needed to:

  • understand the whole process of visa applications, since the new product will be incorporated in an existing environment
  • verify that the problem comes in fact from the biometrics step
  • get into the details and specifics of the problem.

The biometrics step: the most tedious

The observations confirmed that the biometrics step was in fact the one causing the most problems. The agents themsleves didn't really seem to like working at this position. Here are a few findings from our observations:

  • The agents have so many clicks to do, which increases the risk of mistakes.
  • They need to manipulate 2 computers at the same time, one to collect the biometric data, and another to start and stop the recording of the surveillance camera. Nothing is automatic and it happens sometimes that they forget to stop the recording, they end up with a video containing 2 visa applicants. This being totally unacceptable by governments, the only solution is to redo it again.
  • They have to fight against the software many times. For example some people have damaged fingers and in this way cannot have perfect fingerprints; however, the software does not accept fingerprints under a certain quality, a level that is impossible to achieve with damaged fingers. The big problem is that the software does not allow for easy manual overrides.

Service blueprint made by my fellow UX designer after our ethnographic research

Interviewing the visa agents

To get even more qualitative and quantitative data, we chose to interview our users: the agents. I shaped and conducted the interviews with the help of a more experienced designer. We were able to confirm what we observed and we discovered many other things:

  • the old software was ugly but more functional
  • capturing pictures and fingerprints is always faster when it is done manually than automatically by the computer
  • having to scroll makes them lose time
  • parts of the software are never used but they still have to go through them, which makes them lose time

Detailed design

Keeping what is already known

As the main goal here was to improve the productivity, a strategic choice to keep the design pretty similar to the previous softwares was made.
First the users have different levels of confidence with technology, so keeping it similar to what they have been used to is better to not lose time by teaching them to use the software. What we wanted is for them to be able to use it straight away without any prior training.
Also, TLSbiometrics was going to be implemented in another software called TLSdocuments, that the agents already used. We had to fit seamlessly in that software.

Wireframing TLSbiometrics

I took the responsibility of designing the wireframes based on all the research that we made. This was an iterative process during which I was in contact with the agents. The constraints of the project did not allow for time to do usability tests. A choice was made to test later after production. As designers we felt the need to include the users as much as possible to the design process so we did what we could without testing.
